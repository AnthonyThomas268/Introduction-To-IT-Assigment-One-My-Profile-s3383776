My Background

Interest in Information Technology

My interest in IT started in a round about way, I started studying an Associate Degree in Mechanical Engineering at RMIT in 2012 after graduating high school. After two years of study I managed to confirm that I immensely dislike Mechanical Engineering. So, I decided to double down and start another Associate degree in Civil Engineering. After another two years of study I graduated with two Associate Degrees and had confirmed that Engineering may not be the sort of thing I enjoy.

After graduating I lived abroad in Asia, Europe and most recently, North America, where I was working in the Canadian Rockies at a backcountry lodge, taking people on hiking tours in summer and cross-country skiing in winter. When the Covid-19 pandemic hit, I decided to return to Australia when the company I was working for was forced to close its doors and could no longer sponsor my visa.

When I was living in Hanoi, working as an English teacher was where my interest in IT was first sparked. The community of expats in my area were a mix of different professionals, and a small group of them worked in IT; Programmers, Web developers, and other consultants who all had the ability to work remotely and travel to different parts of the world. Seeing the freedom their roles allowed them was very intriguing and I began to consider the possibility of a future in IT.

When I had to relocate back to Melbourne this year, a friend suggested that I apply for an entry level job at their Telcom company, Aussie Broadband. Despite having very little knowledge and no experience in the industry I got a Customer service Sales position. Shortly after starting I was transitioned to working remotely in response to the growing COVID-19 restrictions. Working from home reminded me of the expats in Hanoi, some of who have continued living abroad throughout the pandemic and have managed to maintain stability in their jobs.

Even though my experience of working remotely is from a desk in my flat and not Hanoi, it has me considering a career in IT and how I could expand my role within Aussie Broadband, or even shape a future career around the lifestyle I enjoy, that is challenging, interesting and secure.

I started to take free Programming courses through EDX and Code Academy, as I had always found programming languages interesting and engaging. When the Federal government announced the COVID-19 Undergraduate Certificate courses, I saw that RMIT was offering an Undergraduate certificate in Information Technology. Having been a student at RMIT before, it seemed a natural fit to jump back into higher education to start exploring my options for a career in IT. I am hoping this certificate will give me a broad base of IT knowledge, and better idea of what my options could be moving forward.

    Overview and Motivation

Ideally, this would be developed in conjunction with an existing financial institution, and integrated into their existing services, however it would potentially work as a stand-alone platform if required.

    Despite the massive increase in volume and value of cryptocurrency in recent years, we are yet to see widespread adoption of it as a form of currency to be used in day-to-day life. A report from the Cybersecurity firm Kaspersky found that 81% of people surveyed have never purchased cryptocurrency, and only 6% of people are currently using them. One of the major reasons the lack of widespread adoption suggested is due to the knowledge gap average people have on how to use Cryptocurrency, and a narrow understanding of the technology it uses. (Uncharted territory: why consumers are still wary about adopting cryptocurrency, 2020)

    Mark Cuban has been a long-time critic of cryptocurrency, reasoning that one of the major limiting factors on widespread adoption of it, is that it is simply too hard to use. CSNBC quotes an interview with Cuban From episode 270 of the Pomp Podcast on April 2020 saying “It would have to be completely friction-free and understandable by everybody first.” So easy, in fact, that “grandma could do it.” “You’ve got to be able to spend it, because right now you still have to convert it for anything that you want. And as long as you have to convert it, you’re still dependent on fiat. No matter what you say,” . (Locke, 2020)

    Project Description

    The project would aim to achieve a more widespread use of cryptocurrencies by drastically simplifying the process of transaction with them, as well as incentivising people to hold their wealth in cryptocurrency instead of current popular investment choices. This project would be most effective if it was able to partner with an existing trusted financial institution, such as ING or HSBC. The project could potentially work as a stand-alone platform but there would be significant obstacles to overcome. Partnering with an existing financial institution would add a level of creditability to cryptocurrency and gain trust from customers who may not view it is a legitimate way of performing financial transaction and introduce people who are simply unfamiliar with the technology. This would also help change the very common perception that cryptocurrency is something that “nerds” use, or that it is used exclusively for the purchase nefarious and illegal products/services on the dark web.

    Currently, if somebody wants to make a transaction using cryptocurrency, it is a clunky, tedious, multi-step process. Firstly, the user must purchase their chosen currency, normally through a trading platform, which normally involves putting in credit card details or doing a manual bank transfer to a third party who then passes the money to the trading platform. After this, the purchased currency is transferred to the users soft or hard crypto wallet, and to set up the wallet itself is also tedious often involving a third party. This is far more effort than most people would be willing to go through to conduct a simple transaction. To combat this, our partnering financial institution would be able to implement a series of systems and tools in their existing services and provide consumers with a more streamlined process to use their cryptocurrencies.

    The first step would be to assign each user a crypto wallet, which the bank would handle and maintain all security infrastructure for. If a user already had a crypto wallet, there would be a feature to integrate their current wallet into the system, as well as an option to open additional wallets, much like opening multiple bank accounts under one name. As well as this, customers would now have the incentive of earning interest on the balance of a crypto wallet in the same way their ordinary savings accounts do.

    Making transactions using cryptocurrency would become seamless once the correct systems where introduced, with the ability to convert fiat (government-backed currency like AUD) into cryptocurrency, and vice versa, through your banking app or preferred banking method, much like transferring money from a chequing account to a savings account. This could be easily integrated into consumers daily purchases, for example, when buying your morning coffee with a debit or credit card, you would simply be offered an additional payment option: “Cheque, Savings, Credit or Crypto?”

    This system could potentially be extended into a trading platform as well, that would allow people to purchase stock bonds and other investments using cryptocurrency directly, without having to transfer it into fiat. As time progressed, the systems would be refined, and more features would be added.

    Obstacles and Limitations

    One of the biggest obstacles in getting this project running would be gaining partnership with a trusted and legitimate financial intuition, who are interested and willing to back the concept. Without being partnered with a bank, instead being operated as a standalone platform, this project could be perceived as lacking legitimacy or security by everyday users and may get lost in the noise of other services and trading platforms.

    If developed as a stand-alone platform, the project would be less viable, but not impossible to implement. There would simply be more obstacles to overcome - particularly acquiring the enormous amount of money required to build what would essentially become a bank largely dedicated to dealing with cryptocurrency.

    Once this project was off the ground, it would need the expertise of people from a series of professionals within the finance, telcom and IT industries. The skills required of the IT professionals particularly would vary greatly because of the many facets the project would have while being implemented. It would require data analysts, cyber security specialists, full stack developers, and more. All the hardware required to implement this project is readily available today, and if partnering with an existing financial institution was successful, they would already have most of the tools and professionals with skills required to integrate these tools and systems. Existing banking software and apps could be modified to have these features instead of being developed from scratch.

    Tools, Technologies and Skill Required

    If you were a Full Stack Developer on this project, you would use a variety of tools, such as GitHub as a development platform, Slack, to communicate with people you are working with, MySQL, and many others. You would need to be competent in a variety of programming languages, such as python, JavaScript, CSS, etc.

    Outcome

    If this project was successfully implemented, either as a stand-alone platform or in conjunction with a financial institution, the complication involved in using cryptocurrency would become frictionless and streamlined. We would see faster and more widespread adoption of cryptocurrency, and over time the perception of it would begin to change as more people used it their everyday lives. If enough people started to adopt cryptocurrencies as a preferred method of payment or making transactions, it would shift the current financial system to be more decentralised.
